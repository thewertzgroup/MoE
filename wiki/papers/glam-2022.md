# GLaM: Efficient Scaling of Language Models with Mixture-of-Experts (2022)

> **Title**: GLaM: Efficient Scaling of Language Models with Mixture-of-Experts
> **Authors**: Nan Du, Yanping Huang, Andrew M. Dai, Simon Tong, Dmitry Lepikhin, Yuanzhong Xu, Maxim Krikun, Yanqi Zhou, Adams Wei Yu, Orhan Firat, Barret Zoph, Liam Fedus, Maarten Bosma, Zongwei Zhou, Tao Wang, Yu Emma Wang, Kellie Webster, Marie Pellat, Kevin Robinson, Kathleen Meier-Hellstern, Toju Duke, Lucas Dixon, Kun Zhang, Quoc V. Le, Yonghui Wu, Zhifeng Chen, Claire Cui
> **Year**: 2022
> **Venue**: ICML 2022
> **arXiv**: 2112.06905
> **Significance**: Largest MoE LLM at 1.2T parameters; demonstrated MoE efficiency over dense GPT-3

## Summary

GLaM (Generalist Language Model) scaled MoE to **1.2 trillion parameters** with 64 experts per MoE layer, demonstrating that MoE could match or exceed GPT-3's quality while using **1/3 the training energy** and **1/2 the FLOPs per token**. It was Google's answer to GPT-3, showing that conditional computation was a more efficient path to large-scale language models.

## Architecture

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Total parameters | 1.2 trillion |
| Active parameters per token | 96.6 billion |
| Number of experts per MoE layer | 64 |
| Routing | Top-2 gating |
| Transformer layers | 64 |
| Hidden dimension | 8,192 |
| Attention heads | 128 |
| MoE placement | Every other FFN layer |

### Design Choices

- **Top-2 routing**: Each token processed by its top-2 experts (unlike [Switch Transformer](switch-transformer-2021.md)'s top-1)
- **Every other layer**: Alternates between dense FFN and MoE layers
- **Decoder-only**: Autoregressive language model (like GPT-3)
- **64 experts**: Fewer but larger experts compared to Switch Transformer's 128-2048

## Results

### vs. GPT-3

| Metric | GPT-3 (175B) | GLaM (1.2T) |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Total parameters | 175B | 1.2T |
| Active parameters | 175B | 96.6B |
| Training FLOPs | ~3.14×10²³ | ~1.0×10²³ |
| Training energy | ~1,287 MWh | ~456 MWh |
| Few-shot quality | Baseline | Better on average |

GLaM matched or exceeded GPT-3 on:
- **NLU benchmarks**: SuperGLUE, ANLI, WinoGrande
- **Knowledge tasks**: TriviaQA, Natural Questions
- **Few-shot learning**: Competitive across 29 benchmarks

### Efficiency Headline

> GLaM uses **1/3 the energy** of GPT-3 to train, while achieving **better quality** — the strongest demonstration of MoE efficiency over dense models at the time.

### Scaling Family

GLaM was actually a family of models:
- GLaM (64B/8E) — 64B total, 8 experts
- GLaM (143B/64E) — 143B total, 64 experts  
- GLaM (1.2T/64E) — the flagship

Consistent finding: more experts = better quality per FLOP.

## Training Details

- **Data**: 1.6 trillion tokens from a filtered web corpus + books + Wikipedia
- **Hardware**: 1,024 TPU v4 chips
- **Training time**: ~2.5 months
- **Optimizer**: Adafactor
- **Load balancing**: Auxiliary loss from [Switch Transformer](switch-transformer-2021.md)
- **Capacity factor**: 2.0

## Key Takeaways

1. **MoE > Dense at scale**: GLaM conclusively showed that for a given compute budget, MoE achieves better quality than dense
2. **Environmental argument**: The energy efficiency of MoE was a significant talking point
3. **Practical MoE LLMs**: Demonstrated that MoE works for autoregressive LLMs, not just translation or masked LMs
4. **64 experts is practical**: Showed that you don't need thousands of experts; 64 gives excellent results

## Limitations

- **Not publicly released**: GLaM remained a Google-internal model
- **Inference cost**: 1.2T total parameters requires significant inference infrastructure
- **Less explored fine-tuning**: Limited analysis of downstream fine-tuning behavior

## Impact

GLaM influenced several subsequent developments:
- Validated MoE for large-scale LLMs, inspiring [Mixtral](../models/mixtral.md) and others
- The efficiency argument was instrumental in Google's continued investment in MoE
- Informed the design of subsequent Google models (PaLM had MoE variants internally)
